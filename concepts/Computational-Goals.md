# Computational Goals

## Definition

A Computational Goal is a formalized expression of a desired computation.

Computational goals specify desired future states of knowledge and provide the organizing principle for goal-directed knowledge application.

Examples include:

- Estimating a risk.
- Predicting an outcome.
- Determining a quantity.
- Classifying an entity.
- Selecting among alternatives.
- Generating an explanation.

Computational goals are domain-independent abstractions.

## Why It Matters

Without explicit goals, large collections of computable knowledge appear primarily as collections of artifacts.

When goals are introduced, the same collections may be evaluated in terms of their potential contribution toward realizing desired computations.

Computational goals therefore provide the basis for computational capability discovery and alignment activities.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

Computational Goals are closely related to:

- Desired Future States of Knowledge
- Goal–Knowledge Alignment
- Computable Knowledge
- Computational Capability Discovery

They function as probes through which computational capabilities may be explored.

## Role in the GDIL Research Program

Computational goals are first-class objects within the GDIL Research Program.

Goal representations are central to:

- GDILs
- GARI
- GB-FDOs
- Alignment experiments

Many program activities investigate how different goal representations influence alignment outcomes.
