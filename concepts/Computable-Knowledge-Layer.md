# Computable Knowledge Layer

## Definition

A Computable Knowledge Layer is a conceptual layer consisting of computable knowledge and metadata that collectively support the discovery of computational capabilities.

The layer is not defined by any single repository, system, or implementation. Rather, it emerges from collections of computable knowledge that become sufficiently described, referenced, and connected to support discovery-dependent alignment activities.

A Computable Knowledge Layer is concerned not merely with computable knowledge artifacts themselves, but with the computational capabilities they collectively support.

## Why It Matters

Modern information infrastructure supports discovery of documents, datasets, software, and services.

An equivalent infrastructure for discovering computational capabilities remains underdeveloped.

As computable knowledge accumulates, the challenge shifts from understanding individual artifacts to understanding the computational capabilities present within large bodies of computable knowledge.

The Computable Knowledge Layer concept provides a framework for thinking about this challenge.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

The Computable Knowledge Layer is closely related to:

- Computable Knowledge
- Computational Capability
- Computational Capability Discovery
- Computational Goals
- Goal–Knowledge Alignment

GDILs may be viewed as infrastructure that operates within, upon, or alongside one or more Computable Knowledge Layers.

## Role in the GDIL Research Program

The Computable Knowledge Layer provides the broader conceptual setting within which GDILs operate.

It motivates the research program's central question:

> Given a computational goal and a large universe of computable knowledge, what can be computed?

The GDIL Research Program investigates mechanisms through which the latent computational capabilities of such layers may become observable.
