# Goal-Directed Intelligence Layers (GDILs)

## Definition

Goal-Directed Intelligence Layers (GDILs) are a proposed form of knowledge infrastructure for discovering, aligning, and applying computable knowledge in pursuit of explicit computational goals.

GDILs combine formalized computational goals, computable knowledge, machine-processable metadata, and alignment mechanisms into a common framework that supports goal-directed knowledge application.

The defining intelligence of a GDIL is its ability to align and apply existing computable knowledge in a goal-directed manner.

## Why It Matters

Computable knowledge is increasingly abundant but remains highly distributed across repositories, systems, services, and organizations.

While many mechanisms exist for finding data, documents, software, and services, comparatively little infrastructure exists for determining which computable knowledge may contribute to realization of a particular computational goal.

GDILs address this challenge by introducing explicit computational goals as organizing objects around which computable knowledge can be discovered, aligned, and applied.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

GDILs build upon several foundational concepts:

- Computational Goals
- Desired Future States of Knowledge (DFSKs)
- Computable Knowledge
- Goal–Knowledge Alignment
- Computable Knowledge Layers

GDILs provide the broader infrastructure within which these concepts operate.

## Role in the GDIL Research Program

GDILs are the central scientific object of investigation within the GDIL Research Program.

The program seeks to determine:

- Whether GDILs can be constructed.
- Whether metadata can support effective goal–knowledge alignment.
- Whether large bodies of computable knowledge can be systematically applied to achieve computational goals.
- Whether computational capabilities can become discoverable through alignment processes.
