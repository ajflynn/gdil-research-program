# The GDIL Research Program

**Goal-Directed Intelligence Layers (GDILs)** are a proposed form of knowledge infrastructure for discovering, aligning, and applying computable knowledge in pursuit of explicit computational goals.

This repository is the home of the GDIL Research Program.

---

## Why This Research Exists

Modern computing infrastructure can help us find documents, datasets, software, services, and increasingly generated content.

Yet an important question remains surprisingly difficult to answer:

> **Given a computational goal and a large universe of computable knowledge, what can be computed?**

Computable knowledge already exists throughout the digital ecosystem.

Predictive models, classifiers, calculators, decision rules, workflows, simulations, and other computable artifacts are distributed across repositories, software systems, publications, APIs, and knowledge services. Individually, many of these artifacts are discoverable. Collectively, however, the computational capabilities they support are often hidden.

The GDIL Research Program explores the hypothesis that a new layer of infrastructure can make these capabilities discoverable and usable at scale.

---

## The Core Idea

The World Wide Web transformed access to information by making documents discoverable.

GDILs explore whether a similar transformation is possible for computable knowledge.

Instead of asking:

> "Which documents are relevant?"

GDILs ask:

> "Which computable knowledge may help realize this computational goal?"

This seemingly small shift changes the problem dramatically.

The challenge becomes one of **alignment**.

A computational goal is expressed.

Available computable knowledge is identified.

Potential relationships between the two are determined.

Relevant knowledge is then applied in pursuit of the goal.

## Desired Future States of Knowledge

At the center of the GDIL concept is a simple but powerful idea:

> A computational goal can be specified as a desired future state of knowledge.

Examples include:

| Goal | Desired Future State of Knowledge |
|--------|--------|
| Estimate a risk | The risk estimate is known |
| Classify an entity | The classification is known |
| Determine a quantity | The quantity is known |
| Select among alternatives | The preferred alternative is known |
| Predict an outcome | The predicted outcome is known |

GDILs are conceived as infrastructure for helping realize such desired future states of knowledge through the systematic alignment and application of computable knowledge.

---

## What Is a Goal-Directed Intelligence Layer?

A Goal-Directed Intelligence Layer (GDIL) is a conceptual infrastructure containing:

- Formalized computational goals
- Computable knowledge
- Machine-processable metadata
- Alignment mechanisms
- Supporting reference structures

A GDIL does not generate knowledge from observations.

Instead, it focuses on:

- Discovering available computable knowledge
- Determining its potential relevance to goals
- Aligning computable knowledge with goals
- Supporting the application of computable knowledge

The defining intelligence of a GDIL is its ability to align and apply existing computable knowledge in a goal-directed manner.

## Research Questions

The GDIL Research Program investigates questions such as:

### Computational Capability Discovery

- What computational capabilities exist within large bodies of computable knowledge?
- How can those capabilities be described and discovered?
- Can computational capability itself become an observable property of a knowledge layer?

### Goal–Knowledge Alignment

- To what degree can metadata support alignment between goals and computable knowledge?
- Under which conditions does alignment succeed or fail?
- How should goals and computable knowledge be represented?

### Computable Knowledge Infrastructure

- What metadata are required beyond FAIR metadata?
- What reference structures are needed?
- Can large-scale computable knowledge layers emerge through incremental accumulation?

### Scientific Instrumentation

- How can alignment processes be observed, measured, and studied scientifically?
- What experimental methods support investigation of goal-directed knowledge application?
- What evidence can be generated regarding alignment effectiveness?

## Major Program Components

### Conceptual Foundations

Foundational work defining:

- Goal-Directed Intelligence Layers (GDILs)
- Desired Future States of Knowledge (DFSKs)
- Goal-directed knowledge application
- Computable knowledge layers
- Computational capability discovery

### GARI

The Goal Alignment Research Instrument (GARI) is a scientific instrument for studying goal–knowledge alignment.

GARI supports controlled experiments involving:

- Computational goals
- Computable knowledge
- Alignment engines
- Alignment results
- Constructed realizations

### Canonical Corpora

The program develops experimental corpora including:

- Goal-bearing FAIR Digital Objects (GB-FDOs)
- Computable Biomedical Knowledge-bearing FAIR Digital Objects (CBKB-FDOs)
- Canonical Item Spaces
- Experimental Item Spaces

These corpora support reproducible alignment experiments.

### Specifications and Schemas

Formal specifications define:

- Experimental objects
- Metadata structures
- Reference schemas
- Alignment workflows
- Exchange formats

### Experiments

A growing series of planned experiments investigates:

- Metadata-driven alignment
- Computational capability discovery
- Alignment engine behavior
- Goal representation
- Knowledge representation
- Constructed realization generation

## Long-Term Vision

The long-term vision of this work is not merely to organize computable knowledge.

It is to make the computational capabilities of large bodies of computable knowledge increasingly visible.

In this view, computational goals become probes through which available capability can be explored.

Each alignment exercise reveals something about a specific goal.

Collectively, many alignments reveal something about the capabilities present within an entire layer of computable knowledge.

This leads to a fundamental scientific question:

> If the world's computable knowledge were systematically organized, described, and aligned to explicit goals, what could it compute?

The GDIL Research Program exists to investigate that question.
